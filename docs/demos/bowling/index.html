Bowling for contagion

Companies are pins. Sectors crash together. The covariance can't tell.

A bowling alley is a generative model for a market. Each pin is a company; companies in a sector stand close together, so a shock (a random chord across the table) caroms through and topples them together — default contagion. Throw a few, or hit Fast +1000, and watch two things emerge: the correlation matrix settles down, and the joint-default tail pulls away from the Gaussian copula that very correlation implies — the tail risk a covariance cannot see.

Why this is the point

The correlation matrix on the right is exactly what minimum-variance and Black–Litterman run on. But fit a Gaussian copula to that same correlation and it cannot make a whole sector default at once — so its joint-default tail (the table) falls away from the market's. The cascade is real tail risk that lives above second moments, invisible to any covariance method. A tail-aware overlay races the assets under the actual (cascading) simulation instead of inverting a covariance, so co-defaulters de-duplicate automatically.

Stated plainly, with the honesty the lab demands: de-duplication is a mechanism, not a free lunch. In the bowling lab the full overlay, taken to a standalone portfolio, over-concentrates and worsens the tail; it is a benchmark tilt to dial, which is what the tail-sensitive Black–Litterman demo does on the Dow. The reusable generator and the copula-deviation test are in bowling_sim.py.
