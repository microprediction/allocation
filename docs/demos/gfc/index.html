The Global Financial Crisis

Told through simulation — by someone who built the correlation-trading tools.

The crisis has a popular telling: clever quants, dazzled by an elegant formula, let the maths blind them, and the tails they ignored blew up the world. This essay takes that story apart in stages — with simulations you can run — and replaces it with what actually happened. The arc: first blame the marginal, then blame the dependence, then the truth.

Part 1 · the very confused version
The Normal-distribution fallacy

The loudest version (Taleb's bell-curve "Great Intellectual Fraud," amplified into "the formula that killed Wall Street") blames the Normal distribution. But the structured-credit machinery ran on a Gaussian copula, and the marginals were never a modelling choice at all: each name's default probability was pinned by its CDS spread — the market-implied default probability. The one free knob was the correlation, and you could dial it as high as you liked. By Sklar's theorem the copula carries the dependence entirely separately from those CDS-fixed marginals, so the dramatic shape of a CDO² loss distribution comes from cranking the correlation and the tranching nonlinearity — not from the bell curve. The Normal marginal didn't cause it; the marginals were never Normal and were never the free parameter.

It is worth saying plainly: this strand of the critique never warranted a real response from anyone who did credit analytics for a living. The bell-curve sermon conflated the marginal distribution with the dependence structure the models actually turned on — handwaving from someone who, on the evidence, knew essentially nothing about how these instruments were built. I said so in a short video that went micro-viral and that — to their credit — even Taleb's own admirers enjoyed. The price of admission was a place on Taleb's public "list of smear artists," filed there as "some type of failed quant/mathematician with savantic attributes."

That last phrase was not an accident of temper. The Black Swan leaned throughout on autism as an epithet — science as "an autistic enterprise," the recurring "Dr John" caricature of the literal-minded fool — and I was among the very few at the time to call it for what it was: a brazen attack on autistic people, dressed as iconoclasm. It went entirely unremarked by the Harvard Business Review and every other respectable outlet that lionised the book. Whatever one concludes about copulas, that was a genuinely dark turn in public discourse — worth remembering whenever this particular author is wheeled out as the crisis's moral authority.

Part 2 · the less confused version
Gaussian dependence hid the tail risk

The better story: the Gaussian copula's real, provable sin is zero tail dependence (joint extremes asymptotically vanish for any correlation below 1). Fit it to every pairwise default probability and every marginal and it still can't make a whole sector default at once — so the systemic tail is hidden. That points at tail risk / contagion as the candidate culprit. See the Bowling for contagion demo, which shows exactly this gap.

Part 3 · the narrative arc
Were the quants blinded by the formula?

Here is where the populist story breaks. The legend (canonised in “Recipe for Disaster: The Formula That Killed Wall Street”) is that David Li invented the Gaussian copula around 2000, that quants uncritically lapped it up, and that overconfidence in this beautiful object did the damage. Three things are wrong with that.

One — Li was late. Factor and copula models of correlated default were already in use at multiple banks before the 2000 paper; the latent-Gaussian threshold idea goes back to Merton (1974) and the single-factor portfolio model to Vasicek (1987). The paper named a practice; it did not start one.

Two — the banks were a step ahead: the fast model. Nobody on a desk priced a CDO by brute-force Monte Carlo over a copula. At Morgan Stanley I devised, implemented and deployed the fast semi-analytic version, and — without any doubt — the same happened independently at other banks at much the same time. It had to: the method is forced by the problem. At Morgan Stanley it shipped, gloriously, as an Excel add-in compiled from MATLAB with the old MATLAB compiler — if that doesn't make anyone nostalgic, nothing will.

Three — there was nothing to be dazzled by. Pricing a CDO in the one-factor model is a routine exercise in conditioning, convolution, and quadrature. Condition on the one common factor and the names become independent; convolve their conditional default indicators into a loss distribution; integrate that over the factor by quadrature. A handful of nodes, exact, instant. No Monte Carlo, no mystique. You can run it right here.

Drag ρ: the loss distribution swings from a tight binomial (ρ≈0, defaults roughly independent) to an all-or-nothing bimodal shape (ρ→1, mostly no defaults punctuated by the whole pool going). The thin curves are the conditional loss distributions at three values of the common factor; the bold bars are their quadrature average. Drop the node count and watch how few points it takes to converge — that is the whole "fast model." The senior tranche, riskless-looking at low ρ, becomes a catastrophe bond as ρ rises — the structural fragility, driven entirely by correlation, with the marginal p held fixed. Here p is the CDS-implied default probability — the market-given marginal; on a desk you fit it to the single-name CDS and then turn only ρ.

Part 4 · the model was ugly, not beautiful
Sklar on time, and the hazards that jump

The romance — math professors and disaffected string theorists, dazzled by an elegant object — gets the aesthetics exactly backwards. The construction is ugly. It takes Sklar's theorem (which is just the distributional transform — a near-triviality the conformal-prediction crowd now makes a song and dance about) and staples it onto temporal quantities, the default times. That immediately begs a question the static picture cannot answer: what happens after t > 0?

The only honest move is to condition on what you have learned — which names have defaulted, and when; which survive. But the moment you do, two things go wrong. First, conditioning on survival is a condition on a rectangle in latent space, and the conditional law is no longer a Gaussian copula of the kind you started with — you have left your own model class. Second, the implied hazard rates of the survivors jump — and the jumps are not subtle. Each line is a surviving name's hazard rate as a multiple of its standalone (CDS-implied) rate; the line ends when that name defaults (grey verticals). Between defaults the multiplier drifts below 1 — surviving together is read as good news — then, the instant any name defaults, every survivor's hazard jumps discontinuously upward (at ρ=0.7, by 3–4× on a single default). No real credit reprices like that. This isn't a calibration nuisance; it is the model telling you it has no dynamics.

The same name, two baskets, two hazards

And here is the corollary — obvious once you have seen the jumps. The conditioning that produced them is basket-specific: a survivor's implied hazard depends on the default-and-survival pattern of the other names in its basket. So a single underlying name — and in 2006 a name like Ford or GMAC sat inside dozens of CDOs at once — is handed a different default process by every basket it belongs to. Ask CDO A and CDO B how likely that name is to default tomorrow given what has happened, and they disagree, because each conditions on its own peers. A coherent model attaches an intensity to the name, driven by observable state; the copula attaches it to the basket, and the baskets contradict each other. That inconsistency, not the bell curve, is the genuinely damning property — and it is invisible until you ask the static picture to live through time.

One hedge, two risks

Beneath even that sits a flaw no choice of copula can repair, because it belongs to the market and not the model. A CDO tranche carries (at least) two distinct exposures in each name: default risk — the name suddenly jumps to default — and spread risk — its credit spread drifts and reprices the tranche. But there is exactly one liquid instrument with which to hedge a name: its single-name CDS. One instrument cannot span two independent risk factors, so the market is fundamentally incomplete: the tranche cannot be replicated, there is no unique arbitrage-free price, and the hedge ratios the model reports are artifacts of the model rather than properties of the world. I was not pricing by replication; I was marking to a convention and hoping the unhedgeable residual stayed small. On a desk, everyone knew this too.

Part 5 · the quants knew
You don't start a company to fix something you think is great

The last myth to fall is the one about the people. The story needs the quants to be naive — dazzled, overconfident, blind to the cracks — because a catastrophe caused by a beautiful mistake is a tidier morality tale than one caused by leverage and incentives. But none of the flaws in Part 4 were hidden from the desk. They were obvious to anyone who pushed the model past t = 0, and they were discussed openly among people who, at Morgan Stanley, mostly held PhDs — even the traders had doctorates in statistics or rates.

The dispositive evidence is the simplest kind: you do not start a company to fix something you believe is already great. At the end of 2006 — the issuance machine running flat out, and my warnings going unheeded — I left to found Julius Finance, a company whose entire reason for existing was to fix the credit models. That is not the act of someone seduced by an elegant copula. It is the act of someone who had seen precisely where it broke and could not get anyone to care while the carry was good. The quants were not fooled by the formula. We were ignored — which, as Part 6 argues, is the part of the story that actually matters.
