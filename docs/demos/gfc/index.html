The Global Financial Crisis

The true story of the role of quantitative methods in the financial crisis — told in demonstrations, by someone who led the CDO modelling at Morgan Stanley.

There are three probability measures in finance, not two. Understanding the trichotomy is the key to this whole story.

The crisis has a popular telling: clever quants, dazzled by an elegant formula, let the maths blind them, and the tails they ignored blew up the world. This essay takes that story apart in stages — with simulations you can run — and replaces it with what actually happened. The arc: first blame the marginal, then blame the dependence, then the truth.

The subtitle, I admit, begs an argument from authority. Please ignore it. It does not matter who I am. Go through the arguments here slowly and carefully and either understand them or refute them yourself — that, and not my résumé, is the whole point.

…yes, but if you insist on credentials

All that said: argument from authority is at least the lesser of two evils, the greater being argument from celebrity. So, to cut to the chase — I am the quant from The Big Short. Although, in fairness, I got a gold in the Australian Mathematical Olympiad, not silver — so you will respect my authoritah.

Part 1 · the very confused version that is not worth our time
The Normal-distribution fallacy

The loudest version (Taleb's bell-curve "Great Intellectual Fraud," amplified into "the formula that killed Wall Street") blames the Normal distribution. But the structured-credit machinery ran on a Gaussian copula, and the marginals were never a modelling choice at all: each name's default probability was pinned by its CDS spread — the market-implied default probability. The one free knob was the correlation, and you could dial it as high as you liked. By Sklar's theorem the copula carries the dependence entirely separately from those CDS-fixed marginals, so the dramatic shape of a CDO² loss distribution comes from cranking the correlation and the tranching nonlinearity — not from the bell curve. The Normal marginal didn't cause it; the marginals were never Normal and were never the free parameter.

Want to know what I really think?

I hate spending space on this.

It is worth saying plainly: this strand of the critique never warranted a real response from anyone who did credit analytics for a living. The bell-curve sermon conflated the marginal distribution with the dependence structure the models actually turned on — handwaving from someone who, on the evidence, knew essentially nothing about how these instruments were built. I said so in a video mocking Nassim Taleb's ridiculous, misplaced pomposity that went micro-viral and that — to their credit — even Taleb's own admirers enjoyed. The price of admission was a place on Taleb's public "list of smear artists," filed there as "some type of failed quant/mathematician with savantic attributes." I am, at least, in excellent company: the same list brands the great forecasting scholar Philip Tetlock guilty of "mendacious, perfidious behavior." One takes one's honours where one finds them.

That last phrase was not an accident of temper. The Black Swan leaned throughout on autism as an epithet — science as "an autistic enterprise," the recurring "Dr John" caricature of the literal-minded fool — and I was among the very few at the time to call it for what it was: a brazen attack on autistic people, dressed as iconoclasm. It went entirely unremarked by the Harvard Business Review and every other respectable outlet that lionised the book. Whatever one concludes about copulas, that was a genuinely dark turn in public discourse — worth remembering whenever this particular author is wheeled out as the crisis's moral authority.

To believe The Black Swan you have to believe, in essence, that everyone is an idiot. Many readers met the normal distribution for the first time in its pages and, a moment later, were somehow persuaded they had been handed a second vital fact the experts had overlooked. Life does not work that way. If you think something as really fucking obvious as "the normal distribution has thin tails" had escaped the people who priced this paper for a living — and, as the demonstrations here show, it wasn't even the relevant fact — then you have picked the wrong sucker at the poker table. And if you cannot very quickly spot the real sucker, well, we know how that goes.

You would also have to be blithely unaware of 1987. Black Monday could, by precisely the same logic, have been pinned on Black–Scholes and its constant volatility — and the market's answer was immediate and permanent: the volatility smile, by 2007 a twenty-year-old monument to the plain fact that practitioners already knew returns were not Gaussian and crashes were not rare. The "experts naïvely assumed thin tails" story is borderline plausible before October 1987; after it, it is simply false.

The readers of The Black Swan — politicians, journalists, dignitaries — were the suckers, and a great many put their names in print endorsing a rambling, disorganised slander against autistic people. Perhaps one day I will collate that wall of shame and reproduce it here, and earn a place on a few more lists like Taleb's. (Who, incidentally, keeps a public list of the people he hates on his own website? Who does that?)

Part 2 · the less confused red herring
Did Gaussian dependence hide the tail risk?

The slightly more plausible, but mostly ill-informed, story is the one a numerate critic reaches for: the Gaussian copula has a real, provable sin — zero tail dependence. Joint extremes vanish asymptotically for any correlation below one; fit it to every pairwise default probability and every marginal and it still cannot make a whole sector default at once. That is true, it is elegant, and I find it genuinely interesting — interesting enough that I built a whole demo of it, not to mention invented an entirely new way to put tail risk into any portfolio allocation. The demo shows the copula cannot reproduce a systemic co-default tail no matter how carefully you calibrate the pairs. So tail risk and hidden contagion get nominated as the culprit.

It is the wrong culprit, and I want to be as plain about this as I was about the bell curve. The crisis did not turn on a subtle higher-order property that a covariance cannot see. The failure was far cruder, and entirely first-order: the marginals were wrong — by roughly a factor of two — and the correlation was wrong — by more than a factor of two. When your inputs are off by multiples, whether your copula has exactly the right asymptotic tail is an academic question; you have mispriced the instrument long before any tail subtlety could matter.

How an entire market came to hold assumptions that wrong is the genuinely interesting question — and the answer goes to the very heart of quantitative finance (and, as it happens, to the heart of functional analysis). Here I am going to do something I do not believe anyone in finance has done in print before: insist that there are three important probability measures, not two.

Watch the ladder. Newcomers — most of the machine-learning crowd now wading in — think there is only one measure: objective, physical probability, the frequency with which things actually happen. The more sophisticated know there are two: that real-world measure, and the risk-neutral measure under which discounted prices are martingales and derivatives are valued. Even Taleb is comfortably smart enough to know there are two.

But to understand the crisis you need a third — and, save for one genuinely obscure blog post from about fifteen years ago that I can barely turn up myself, I am not aware of anyone who will tell you it exists. This is not a conspiracy. It is just that, having watched an adjacent desk lose some nine billion dollars on a single trade (the full story, and its place among the ugliest trades ever made, are worth your time), I happen to have spent more time reflecting on this than most — and I think the P / Q / R trichotomy is probably the cleanest pedagogical device for it. I will return to the true story of the relationship between copulas and the famous ten-billion-dollar trade in due course — but we have some preliminaries. I will name the third measure, and show how its conflation with the other two is what put the marginals off by a factor of two and the correlation off by more than that — carefully, further down (Parts 6 and 8).

Taleb, by the way, knows all this as well as most — his early, seriously quantitative derivatives books, Dynamic Hedging (1997) chief among them, are real practitioner's texts, and he made genuinely useful contributions long before he accidentally authored the worst mis-explanation in financial history. He was, apparently, too taken with the delicious curves of the Gaussian distribution to think outside the box and finger measure choice as the real culprit.

But first we have to dispense, quickly, with the obvious nonsense — the bell curve here, the elegant-but-irrelevant tail-dependence story just now — so that the real account has clean ground to stand on. I shall not apologize. It is not my fault that a reasonable amount needs to be un-learned before the actual explanation can breathe.

Part 3 · the narrative arc
Were the quants blinded by the formula?

Here is where the populist story breaks. The legend (canonised in “Recipe for Disaster: The Formula That Killed Wall Street”) is that David Li invented the Gaussian copula around 2000, that quants uncritically lapped it up, and that overconfidence in this beautiful object did the damage. Three things are wrong with that.

One — Li was late. Factor and copula models of correlated default were already in use at multiple banks before the 2000 paper; the latent-Gaussian threshold idea goes back to Merton (1974) and the single-factor portfolio model to Vasicek (1987). The paper named a practice; it did not start one.

Two — the banks were a step ahead: the fast model. Nobody on a desk priced a CDO by brute-force Monte Carlo over a copula. At Morgan Stanley I devised, implemented and deployed the fast semi-analytic version, and — without any doubt — the same happened independently at other banks at much the same time. It had to: the method is forced by the problem. I shipped it, gloriously, as an Excel add-in compiled from MATLAB with the old MATLAB compiler — if that doesn't make anyone nostalgic, nothing will. (Btw, try getting that to work across sixty different installations of Office in twelve different business groups and you will understand why there might never be a version 1.1.)

Three — there was nothing to be dazzled by. Pricing a CDO in the one-factor model is a routine exercise in conditioning, convolution, and quadrature. (Yeah, I could do that without anyone telling me to, because, you know, I'm a QUANTITATIVE.) Condition on the one common factor and the names become independent; convolve their conditional default indicators into a loss distribution; integrate that over the factor by quadrature. A handful of nodes, exact, instant. No Monte Carlo, no mystique. You can run it right here.

One distraction to set aside: these subordination games are not path-dependent in any non-trivial way. A vanilla tranche is a call spread on the terminal cumulative loss, so only the terminal loss distribution matters and the quadrature above suffices — when the losses arrive is irrelevant. Path-dependence enters only once you bolt on structural bells and whistles whose cashflows depend on loss timing — cash-reserve accounts, excess-spread and principal/interest waterfalls, OC/IC triggers, turbo amortization — at which point you fall back to simulating paths (the Monte-Carlo-over-a-copula route à la Li) — unless, of course, you are cleverer than the average bear (read on) and SUPER QUANTITATIVE. Do not import that path intuition into the plain case, where it does not belong.

Drag ρ: the loss distribution swings from a tight binomial (ρ≈0, defaults roughly independent) to an all-or-nothing bimodal shape (ρ→1, mostly no defaults punctuated by the whole pool going). The thin curves are the conditional loss distributions at three values of the common factor; the bold bars are their quadrature average. Drop the node count and watch how few points it takes to converge — that is the whole "fast model." The senior tranche, riskless-looking at low ρ, becomes a catastrophe bond as ρ rises — the structural fragility, driven entirely by correlation, with the marginal p held fixed. Here p is the CDS-implied default probability — the market-given marginal; on a desk you fit it to the single-name CDS and then turn only ρ.

Part 4 · the model was ugly, not beautiful
Sklar on time, and the hazards that jump

The romance — math professors and disaffected string theorists, dazzled by an elegant object — gets the aesthetics exactly backwards. Physicists and mathematicians are raised on actual beauty, and the odds of one of them finding a CDO model elegant are about those a rating agency would assign to a super-senior tranche taking a loss. The construction is ugly. It takes Sklar's theorem (which is just the distributional transform — a near-triviality the conformal-prediction crowd now makes a song and dance about) and staples it onto temporal quantities, the default times. That immediately begs a question the static picture cannot answer: what happens after t > 0?

The only honest move is to condition on what you have learned — which names have defaulted, and when; which survive. But the moment you do, two things go wrong. First, conditioning on survival is a condition on a rectangle in latent space, and the conditional law is no longer a Gaussian copula of the kind you started with — you have left your own model class. Second, the implied hazard rates of the survivors jump — and the jumps are not subtle. Below, a five-name basket: simulate the default times, then watch each surviving name's hazard, as a multiple of its standalone rate, walk through time and lurch at every default.

correlation ρ:

Each line is a surviving name's hazard rate as a multiple of its standalone (CDS-implied) rate; the line ends when that name defaults (grey verticals). Between defaults the multiplier drifts below 1 — surviving together is read as good news — then, the instant any name defaults, every survivor's hazard jumps discontinuously upward (at ρ=0.7, by 3–4× on a single default). No real credit reprices like that. This isn't a calibration nuisance; it is the model telling you it has no dynamics.

Now, in fairness, there is more to the story. You can, if you wish, enrich the filtration — the quant's term for telling the model more as time passes: partial information about the deteriorating credit of a name that has not yet defaulted. There is even a graceful — too strong a word — way to marry the direct modelling of instantaneous default intensities (stochastic hazard rates: jump processes, affine models, regime-switching) to the copula. You let the copula generate the exponential random variables, then run the integrated stochastic hazard ∫₀ᵗ λ(s) ds and wait for it to catch up to each drawn exponential — whoever's integral arrives first defaults first. That awkwardly unifies the two warring halves of the 1990s credit literature — reduced-form intensities and copulas — and at Morgan Stanley we even patented it. (A demo of that time-change construction may follow.) None of it, however, removes the inescapable ugliness we come to next.

The same name, two baskets, two hazards

And here is the corollary — obvious once you have seen the jumps. The conditioning that produced them is basket-specific: a survivor's implied hazard depends on the default-and-survival pattern of the other names in its basket. So a single underlying name — and in 2006 a name like Ford or GMAC sat inside dozens of CDOs at once — is handed a different default process by every basket it belongs to. Ask CDO A and CDO B how likely that name is to default tomorrow given what has happened, and they disagree, because each conditions on its own peers. A coherent model attaches an intensity to the name, driven by observable state; the copula attaches it to the basket, and the baskets contradict each other. That inconsistency, not the bell curve, is the genuinely damning property — and it is invisible until you ask the static picture to live through time.

One hedge, two risks

I will not dwell on the next point, because it is thoroughly well appreciated and I have nothing original to add to it — but it has to be stated. Beneath everything above sits a flaw no choice of copula can repair, because it belongs to the market and not the model. A CDO tranche carries (at least) two distinct exposures in each name: default risk — the name suddenly jumps to default — and spread risk — its credit spread drifts and reprices the tranche. But there is exactly one liquid instrument with which to hedge a name: its single-name CDS. One instrument cannot span two independent risk factors, so the market is fundamentally incomplete: the tranche cannot be replicated, there is no unique arbitrage-free price, and the hedge ratios the model reports are artifacts of the model rather than properties of the world (the standard texts make this plain — Schönbucher 2003; Bielecki & Rutkowski 2002 — and the hedging literature confirms the ratios are model-dependent: Cont & Kan 2011; Laurent, Cousin & Fermanian 2011). I was not pricing by replication; I was marking to a convention and hoping the unhedgeable residual stayed small. On a desk, everyone knew this too.

If you want a single date when the market admitted as much, take May 2005. The downgrade of General Motors and Ford to junk detonated the fashionable "correlation trade" — long the equity tranche, short the mezzanine — and as positions were unwound the implied correlations of different tranches slipped in incompatible directions, driven by raw supply and demand rather than by anything a single coherent model could produce. The clinical study of the episode (Acharya, Schaefer & Zhang, 2015) shows the co-movement spike was a transient, sharply mean-reverting artifact of dealer-quote imbalances and liquidity — not a change in any fundamental correlation. The quoted correlation was revealed for what it was: a technicals-driven price, not a parameter (BIS Quarterly Review, March and June 2005; IMF Global Financial Stability Report, April 2006).

Base correlation: the kludge that became a market standard

If you still suspect the practitioners were seduced by the elegance of all this, let me introduce the single most damning piece of evidence to the contrary — the thing the market actually quoted.

The natural idea is compound (or implied) correlation: take a tranche's market price and back out the single Gaussian-copula correlation that reproduces it, exactly as you read implied volatility out of an option. It does not work. A mezzanine tranche's expected loss is not monotone in correlation — it rises, then falls — so a given market price can correspond to two correlations, or to none at all. The most important parameter in the model is not even a well-defined function of the price. Drag the market price below and watch the line cut the mezzanine curve twice.

So J.P. Morgan published a fix (McGinty, Beinstein, Ahluwalia & Watts, Introducing Base Correlations, 2004), and it became the market quoting convention almost overnight. The trick: never quote the tranche [a, d] directly. Decompose it into two base tranches off zero — [0, d] minus [0, a] — because a base (equity-like) tranche's loss is monotone in correlation, so each inverts to a unique base correlation. Bootstrap those upward across detachment points and you have a tidy, quotable number for every tranche.

Want to know what I really think?

I will be honest: base correlation made me want to puke. Look at what it actually is. It is a change of coordinates chosen for no reason other than to make a broken model emit a single number. The base correlations you back out are not equal across detachments — they form a rising skew — which, if the single-correlation Gaussian copula were true, could not happen: one model, one correlation. The skew is the model screaming that it is wrong, and base correlation's contribution was to write the scream down neatly and call it a quote. That is the opposite of being seduced by elegance. It is a profession holding its nose and agreeing on a common lie so that trades can clear. Everyone knew. We quoted it anyway.

Left — compound correlation: expected loss of the 3–7% mezzanine vs ρ is a hump, so your market-price line crosses it twice (or not at all). Which one is "the correlation"? There is no answer. Right — base correlation: the [0–3%] and [0–7%] base tranches are each monotone, so any level inverts to a single β — but the two βs differ, and that gap is the base- correlation skew a true one-correlation model could never produce. The kludge is well-posed and dishonest at the same time.

Part 5 · the quants knew
You don't start a company to fix something you think is great

The last myth to fall is the one about the people. The story needs the quants to be naive — dazzled, overconfident, blind to the cracks — because a catastrophe caused by a beautiful mistake is a tidier morality tale than one caused by leverage and incentives. But none of the flaws in Part 4 were hidden from the desk. They were obvious to anyone who pushed the model past t = 0. You would have to imagine that, to a quant, the model was some black box or forbidding piece of machinery — whereas in fact its moving parts could not have been simpler. The Bayesian implications were obvious to me, if not to the desk or to management, and I presented them in 2002.

Lest this come out the wrong way: the point would certainly not have been lost on the interest-rate traders with PhDs in statistics. Credit, though, was a newer and rather different game, one in which regulatory, legal and ratings arbitrage were first-class components rather than afterthoughts — and any plausible social model of who ends up where would tilt the Bayesian posterior on mathematical sophistication accordingly.

The dispositive evidence is the simplest kind: you do not start a company to fix something you believe is already great. At the end of 2006 — the issuance machine running flat out, and my warnings going unheeded — I began my first entrepreneurial journey, founding Julius Finance, a company whose entire reason for existing was to fix the credit models. That is not the act of someone seduced by an elegant copula. It is the act of someone who had seen precisely where it broke and could not get anyone to care while the carry was good.

Part 6 · the truth
What actually happened

"Ignored" is a bit strong. The warnings landed — they simply changed nothing, because nothing in the incentive structure rewarded acting on them. The carry was good, the capital rules did not charge for the systematic tail, and a flaw everyone could see was a flaw nobody was paid to fix.

Part of it was organisational. Banks run in cycles, with power swinging back and forth between the trading desks and the risk groups, and I probably don't have to tell you which way that pendulum was swinging in 2004 and 2005. It is not hard to imagine that at some banks a PhD mathematician ended up with a dotted reporting line to the most junior trader on a desk — rather than to an experienced practitioner who had lived through a crisis or two (and who, as it happens, also held a doctorate; my own manager had two). When the people who can see the flaw report to the people being paid not to, the flaw is safe.

None of which is to say the desks were blind to the value of a model, or unwilling to credit the people who helped them understand the shifting combinatorics of the CDO market — and profit handsomely from it. They were neither.

By chance I once fell into an off-hand conversation with the senior person who ran the entire derivative-products business — I'm fairly sure it was around 2003, because it felt like about a year into my ranting against my own model. He was genuinely curious, and I walked him through the flaws. He asked exactly the right question — what would the fix be? — and I gave him the philosophically obvious if technically forbidding answer: build applied-mathematical methods that trace the dynamics of a CDO through time in a way that is at once self-consistent and market-consistent. That conversation spun up a dedicated research effort inside Morgan Stanley, probably unlike most on the Street. Its output was never published, but it made real inroads: drastically lower computation time, and dynamic modelling of CDOs — and even CDO-squareds (their own demo, to come) — that was reasonably fast, reasonably plausible, and reasonably well calibrated. The firm was not ignoring the problem. Parts of it were quietly trying to solve it.

The diversity score: a heuristic that hides the only lever that matters

A favourite defence of the agencies is that they never touched these fancy copulas — they used simpler, supposedly sturdier non-models. Moody's flagship was the Binomial Expansion Technique: take a pool of N correlated names, replace it with D independent identical names — the famous diversity score — and read the loss off a binomial. D comes out smaller than N because names in the same industry are deemed to count for less, via a fixed table. It sounds prudent; it is the opposite of robust. The diversity score is a function of how many industries the pool spans and almost nothing else, so it bakes a single, fixed, invisible correlation into the rating and gives you no way to move it. The most important number in the whole problem is welded shut and painted the colour of the wall.

Drag the copula's correlation: more clustering, a fatter loss tail, a senior tranche that slides from safe to ruined. Now watch the diversity-score curve — it never moves. The Binomial Expansion Technique has no correlation input; the diversity score fixed it (here a low, frozen ≈5%) the instant the pool's industries were counted, and there is no dial to turn. That is what "robust non-model" meant in practice: a model that cannot be wrong about correlation because it is not permitted to have an opinion that changes. Coval–Jurek–Stafford showed the resulting AAA was extraordinarily fragile to exactly the input the heuristic had hidden.

None of this exonerates the copula — fed a low, stable correlation, it was wrong too. But there is a world of difference between getting a number wrong out loud, where it can be stressed and re-marked, and refusing to let the number exist at all. When correlation finally marched toward one in 2008, the diversity score had no channel to say so — Moody's diversity scores were, if anything, being raised even as the system seized — so the ratings did not degrade gracefully; they fell off a cliff of mass downgrades. Moody's later abandoned the zero-residual-correlation assumption altogether and moved to an explicit Gaussian-copula model. The remediation was, precisely, to stop hiding the lever.

In the land of the blind

And here is the subtler failure — the one that survives even when an agency did reach for a copula. They fed it the wrong correlation, taken from historical, actuarial studies, and the numbers were strikingly low.

The asset correlation — the dependence between the latent variables driving default — was modest: S&P's CDO Evaluator assumed 0% across industries before late 2005 (30% within); Moody's used roughly 3% across sectors (15% within).

The default correlation — the chance two names actually go down together, naturally a small number — was lower still and demonstrably too low: Nickerson & Griffin (2017) find agencies assumed about 1% for CLOs (raised to a still-inadequate 3% after the crisis) where roughly 12% was appropriate.

Meanwhile, two screens away, the inter-dealer market in CDO tranches was quoting prices that implied a far higher correlation. Implied correlation is the number you must put into the model to reproduce a traded price — exactly as implied volatility is read out of option prices.

It is a flawed lens. Like the options smile, different tranches imply different correlations, so it is not globally consistent. But it is absolutely critical, because it is the market's own view of joint-default risk, priced by people with money at stake — the one-eyed man in the land of the blind. The agencies stared at their single-digit actuarial numbers and looked away from him (the sensitivity of senior-tranche ratings to exactly this input is the thesis of Coval, Jurek & Stafford 2009).

Ignoring a live market price in favour of a stale historical estimate is not a venial sin; it is close to the cardinal one. I have since argued that this sin needs a name — the Indispensable Markets Hypothesis: in the presence of a well-established market, anyone who eschews its prices as inputs will under-perform out of sample (working paper). The cleanest evidence came later, from the M6 forecasting competition: hundreds of data scientists chasing $300,000 in prizes, and a lazy read of the options market beat 94% of them. If the market routinely out-forecasts the modellers when the modellers have the data, imagine the self-inflicted wound of rating a half-trillion-dollar market off historical tables while the implied number sat, ignored, on the next screen.

Part 7 · twenty years later
Symmetry, de Finetti, and an unexpected second life

Indulge me for a moment

I will return to the central theme — incorrect inputs and the violation of the Indispensable Markets Hypothesis — in due course. But first, I am sure you are dying to know what became of all that hard work. Oh, you're not? Well, too bad. I have the mic just now, and having listened to twenty years of mostly bullshit, I don't much care to be interrupted.

Twenty years on, I find myself tempted to recreate that work. It is work that, as far as I know, has never been independently discovered. It rested on two ideas I still think are important: the symmetries of a CDO, and de Finetti-style decompositions of the joint default distribution. I will fold the full account into this essay sooner or later; here is the spine of it.

The symmetry answers an objection you have probably been forming. The companies in a CDO are plainly not exchangeable — Ford is not GMAC is not a Florida homebuilder. True. But the CDO's cashflows are. The waterfall pays out of aggregate losses; it never mentions a company by name. The payout is a symmetric function of the default vector — invariant under any permutation of the names. And that lets you move the symmetry from one side of the problem to the other: for the sole purpose of simulating CDO cashflows, there is always a purely exchangeable default model that reproduces exactly the same cashflow distribution as the original heterogeneous one. The heterogeneity that felt essential is, for pricing, something you can symmetrize away losing nothing.

Once the defaults are exchangeable, de Finetti — or, in the finite case, Szekely's — decomposition applies: an exchangeable law is a mixture of conditionally-independent laws. The diversity score was a crude, frozen shadow of exactly this (replace N heterogeneous names with D exchangeable ones) collapsed into a single number instead of an exact, dynamic decomposition. Done properly, that decomposition is the natural language for tracing CDO dynamics through time, self- and market-consistently.

The deep version of this is, oddly, having a second life. I keep noticing that conformal prediction — which I never paid much attention to — is in some sense a univariate cousin of the CDO analytics from 2000: both turn on exchangeability and the distributional transform. Technical readers might (I say might) be interested in a new result (SSRN 6979679) and the work in progress at conformalprediction.net — in particular the de Finetti / Feynman piece and a demo of the finite (Szekely) decomposition of exchangeable variables. The topic is deep, and the connections keep multiplying.

The same trick, during COVID of all things

I came back to this from an unlikely direction. In 2020 I noticed the identical move solves a problem in epidemiology, and wrote up a "Fundamental Theorem of Epidemiology" — and, with it, a clean resolution of the herd-immunity paradox. The link is not really a coincidence: epidemiological compartmental models are negative-interest-rate models (and often bad ones, with flat term structures) — a correspondence that seems to have gone unremarked until my working papers pointed it out. If you care about my meagre contributions there, see repeat contacts and the spread. The reason the trick carries over is exactly the symmetry above. Suppose you only care about an aggregate — the number of infections after three days, say, or the number of defaults in a pool. That quantity is a symmetric function of who-is-who, so it cannot tell nature's true (heterogeneous) model apart from its symmetrized twin, nor from any model sharing the same orbit under relabelling:

nature's true model𝔼φ*[ i(t=3) ] = nature's symmetrized model𝔼ψ=Uφ*[ i(t=3) ] = any model in the same orbit𝔼φ∈O(φ*)[ i(t=3) ]

So you will accidentally get the right answer, as it were, as long as you are close to the orbit — and you may as well do your computation on the simplest representative of it: the exchangeable one.

See it: a heterogeneous pool and its exchangeable twin

Three names with different default probabilities and a positive correlation. The left panel is nature's heterogeneous joint distribution over all eight default outcomes; the middle panel is its symmetrization — average over the six relabellings — which is perfectly exchangeable (its three marginals are now identical, and every outcome with the same number of defaults is equally likely). They are visibly different distributions. Yet the right panel — the distribution of the number of defaults, which is all a CDO waterfall ever sees — is bit-for-bit identical under the two models. Drag the sliders; the two aggregate distributions move in perfect lockstep, because one is the permutation-average of the other and the aggregate cannot tell them apart.

This is not special to the Gaussian copula — it holds for any default model whatsoever, because it is a statement about the payout's symmetry, not the model's form. It is also the honest version of the diversity score: not "pretend the names are identical," but "replace them with the exact exchangeable law that prices identically, then decompose that."

"Negative energies and probabilities should not be considered as nonsense. They are well-defined concepts mathematically, like a negative of money." — P. A. M. Dirac, 1942

A further aside, for the connoisseur. The finite de Finetti / Szekely decomposition writes the exchangeable law as a mixture of conditionally-independent ones — and in general the mixing weights are a signed measure: you may have to dip into the negative-probability region to represent it, which, pace Dirac, is perfectly respectable. But here is the quiet gift of this particular problem: you generally do not need to. When defaults are positively correlated — as credit defaults overwhelmingly are — the decomposition stays inside the genuine, non-negative simplex. That is the benefit of positive correlation in defaults. (Then there is the Wigner quasiprobability distribution… for another day.)

Part 8 · how the trick was actually used
Back to the narrative in which Taleb is a genius and the rest of us are morons

Anyway — back to the narrative in which Taleb is a genius and the rest of us are morons. You might reasonably ask: this lovely computational trick, ignored to this day and applicable to every model rather than just the Gaussian copula — did Morgan Stanley race to weaponise the secret sauce? Did the traders with PhDs in statistics fall upon it? Here the true story — not the one inferred by outsiders from a comfortable distance — takes a more curious turn.

By this point the pendulum had swung hard toward origination. Consider the economics: a bank could assemble a CDO, sell n−1 slices of the pie for roughly the price of the whole pie, and keep the n-th slice almost for free. Maybe the free slice ends up worthless; maybe it doesn't. Either way the only quantity anyone is really optimising is how fast you can originate the next one. Take the money and run. In that climate, an exact, elegant method for tracing CDO dynamics through time is not a competitive advantage anyone is clamouring for. It is overhead.

And it was right around then that I wandered over to the desk of the man who would become my next manager, to ask what he needed. The timing, as it turned out, was auspicious. He was setting out on the most ambitious use I ever saw of the very arbitrage I have been describing — the gap between the rating agencies' actuarial default probabilities and the risk-neutral probabilities the market was actually quoting.
